Resident Tax in Japan: Why It Often Starts Later

Resident tax is one of the most confusing salary deductions in Japan because it is usually based on your previous year’s income and paid to your local municipality.

Quick answer

Resident tax is a local tax paid to the city, ward, town, or village where you live. It is often calculated based on your income from the previous year. Because of this timing, people who start working in Japan may not see resident tax immediately, but may start paying it later.

Why it feels delayed

Resident tax often does not feel like an immediate tax. That is because the tax amount is usually calculated after the year ends, based on income information from the previous year.

This creates a timing gap:

Year 1
You earn income in Japan.
After Year 1
Your income information is reported and processed.
Year 2
Resident tax based on Year 1 income may start being collected.

How resident tax is paid

Special collection

Your employer deducts resident tax from your salary and pays it to the municipality. This is common for employees.

Ordinary collection

You receive tax bills from the municipality and pay them yourself. This may happen in some situations.

The method can depend on your employment status, timing, and municipality procedures.

Why your take-home pay may drop

If resident tax was not deducted during your first months or first year, your take-home pay may feel higher at first. Later, when resident tax starts, your monthly take-home pay may become lower even if your gross salary did not change.

🐱 Cat tip: A lower second-year take-home pay does not always mean your salary went down. Resident tax may have started.

Common misunderstandings

“Resident tax is the same as income tax.”

No. Income tax is national. Resident tax is local and paid to your municipality.

“If I did not pay it last month, I will never pay it.”

Not necessarily. It may start later because it is based on previous-year income.

“My company made a mistake because my pay suddenly dropped.”

Maybe not. Check whether resident tax started appearing on your payslip.

“I can ignore the bills if I move.”

No. If you receive official tax bills, you should confirm and pay them properly.

What to check

  • Your payslip: is there a line for 住民税?
  • Your mailbox: did your municipality send payment slips?
  • Your company: is resident tax handled by salary deduction?
  • Your moving date: did you move to a different municipality?
  • Your first year in Japan: resident tax timing may be different from later years.

Summary

Resident tax in Japan is confusing mostly because of timing. It is local tax, often based on previous-year income, and may be paid through your salary or by payment slips. If your take-home pay drops in your second year, resident tax may be one reason.
