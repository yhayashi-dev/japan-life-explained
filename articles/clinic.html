How to Visit a Clinic in Japan

Visiting a clinic in Japan is usually organized and practical, but the reception process, insurance check, and prescription system can feel confusing the first time.

Quick answer

When visiting a clinic in Japan, bring your health insurance proof, Residence Card, cash or payment method, and any medication information. At reception, you may fill out a medical questionnaire, wait, see the doctor, pay at the counter, and then take your prescription to a pharmacy.

Before you go

Many clinics accept walk-ins, but some require reservations. It is safer to check the clinic website or call first if you can. Some clinics have specific hours for first-time patients.

Check the department
For example: internal medicine, dermatology, ophthalmology, dentistry, orthopedics, or ENT.
Check clinic hours
Many clinics close during lunch and may have different hours by day.
Bring documents
Insurance proof, Residence Card, and medication information are useful.
Prepare simple symptom words
Writing symptoms on your phone can help if Japanese is difficult.

What to bring

  • Health insurance card, My Number health insurance card, or eligibility document
  • Residence Card
  • Cash or payment method
  • Medication notebook, if you have one
  • List or photo of medicines you currently take
  • Referral letter, if another doctor gave you one
  • Your address and phone number

At reception

At the clinic reception desk, staff usually check your insurance information and ask whether it is your first visit. You may receive a medical questionnaire called 問診票.

The questionnaire may ask about your symptoms, allergies, current medicines, medical history, pregnancy status, and emergency contact.

After seeing the doctor

After the examination, you usually return to the waiting area and wait for payment. If medicine is needed, the clinic may give you a prescription. In many cases, you take the prescription to a pharmacy nearby.

🐱 Cat tip: In Japan, the clinic and pharmacy are often separate. Do not be surprised if you pay at the clinic and then pay again at the pharmacy.

Common misunderstandings

“Hospital and clinic are the same.”

Not exactly. Clinics are often smaller and easier for common symptoms. Large hospitals may require referrals or extra fees in some cases.

“I can get medicine directly from the doctor.”

Sometimes, but often you receive a prescription and take it to a pharmacy.

“Insurance makes the visit free.”

No. Insurance usually reduces the cost, but you often still pay a portion at the counter.

“Every clinic accepts foreign languages.”

No. Some clinics offer English support, but many do not. Simple written notes can help.

Summary

To visit a clinic in Japan, prepare your insurance proof, ID, payment method, and symptom information. Expect a reception check, questionnaire, waiting time, doctor visit, payment, and possibly a separate pharmacy visit. If Japanese is difficult, simple notes and key phrases can make the visit much easier.
