Why Is My Take-home Pay Lower Than My Salary in Japan?

Your salary offer or contract usually shows gross salary. The money that arrives in your bank account is take-home pay after taxes and social insurance deductions.

Quick answer

In Japan, your monthly salary usually has several deductions before you receive it. Common deductions include income tax, resident tax, health insurance, pension, employment insurance, and sometimes company-specific items. Your bank transfer amount is the final amount after those deductions.

The basic idea

When people first work in Japan, the most surprising thing is often that the salary amount written in the contract is not the amount paid into the bank account. This is normal. The contract amount is usually gross salary, and the transfer amount is net pay or take-home pay.

Think of your salary in three layers:

1. Earnings
Base salary, overtime pay, commuting allowance, and other payments.
2. Deductions
Taxes, social insurance, pension, and other amounts subtracted.
3. Final payment
The amount actually transferred to your bank account.

Common deductions

Income tax

A national tax withheld from your salary. The amount depends on income and other conditions.

Resident tax

A local tax paid to your municipality. It often starts later, so it can surprise people in their second year.

Health insurance

Insurance that helps reduce medical costs when you visit clinics or hospitals.

Pension

Public pension contribution. It is often one of the larger monthly deductions.

Employment insurance

A smaller deduction related to unemployment and employment support programs.

Company-specific items

Some companies may deduct cafeteria fees, union fees, housing, or other items if applicable.

Why resident tax surprises people

Resident tax is often based on your income from the previous year. Because of that, some new workers may not see much resident tax at first, but it may appear later. This timing is one of the most common surprises for foreign residents and new employees.

🐱 Cat tip: If your second-year take-home pay suddenly feels lower, resident tax may be one reason.

Common misunderstandings

“My company paid me less than promised.”

Maybe not. First compare gross salary, deductions, and final payment on your payslip.

“Resident tax is a mistake because it started later.”

Not necessarily. Resident tax timing often depends on previous-year income and municipality billing.

“Commuting allowance is always tax-free.”

Not always in every situation. There are rules and limits, so check your payslip and company policy.

“All deductions are tax.”

No. Some are taxes, and some are social insurance or other items.

What to check on your payslip

  • Gross salary or total earnings
  • Overtime pay and allowances
  • Income tax
  • Resident tax
  • Health insurance
  • Pension
  • Employment insurance
  • Final payment amount

If the payslip is confusing, start by finding the final payment amount and then work backward through the deductions.

Summary

Your take-home pay in Japan is lower than your gross salary because taxes and social insurance are deducted before payment. The key is to separate your payslip into earnings, deductions, and final payment. Resident tax timing can also make your pay feel different after the first year.
