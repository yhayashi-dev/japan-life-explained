The Bank & Phone Loop in Japan

In Japan, opening a bank account and getting a mobile phone number can feel like a loop. A bank may ask for a phone number, while a phone company may ask for a bank account or Japanese payment method.

Quick answer

The loop happens because banks, phone companies, and payment services all want a way to confirm your identity, contact you, and collect payment. The easiest way out is usually to prepare your Residence Card, registered address, contact method, and payment option before applying.

This is why people sometimes feel stuck: each service asks for something from another service.

Why this happens

For many services in Japan, companies need to know where you live, how to contact you, and how you will pay. If one of those pieces is missing, the application may become harder.

  • Banks may want a Japanese phone number so they can contact you or verify your application.
  • Mobile phone companies may want a bank account, credit card, or payment method.
  • Online services may ask for SMS verification, which requires a usable phone number.
  • Some applications may be harder if your address is not registered yet.

A practical order that may help

1. Register your address
Go to your city or ward office after moving in and register your address on your Residence Card.
2. Prepare a contact method
If possible, use a temporary phone plan, prepaid SIM, eSIM, or contact number accepted by the service.
3. Try a bank that accepts your situation
Some banks are easier for new residents, students, or employees than others.
4. Set up phone payment
Once you have a bank account or accepted card, phone contracts and online services become easier.

Common misunderstandings

“I cannot do anything without a bank account.”

Not always. Some services may accept a credit card, prepaid option, or another payment method.

“All banks have the same rules.”

No. Requirements can differ by bank, branch, visa status, length of stay, employer, and available documents.

“A foreign phone number should be enough.”

Sometimes it is not enough. Many Japanese services expect a Japanese phone number for contact or SMS verification.

“If one company says no, everything is impossible.”

No. A different bank, phone plan, or order of applications may work better.

What to prepare before applying

  • Residence Card
  • Address already registered at the city or ward office
  • Passport
  • Student ID, employee ID, or work/school document if you have one
  • A contact method such as a phone number or email address
  • A payment method, if available

Requirements differ, so check the company’s official instructions before going.

🐱 Cat tip: The loop feels personal, but it is usually just paperwork logic. Change the order, prepare documents, and try again.

Summary

The bank and phone loop is common for new residents in Japan. It happens because each service wants identity, address, contact, and payment information. Register your address first, prepare documents, and build the pieces one by one.
